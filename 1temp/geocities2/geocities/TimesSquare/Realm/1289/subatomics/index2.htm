 Subatomic Particles 

 The universe we live in is a mysterious place, which stretches in all directions and is made out of little, tinny particles called subatomic particles. Some of these subatomic particles are called Quarks. They are among the most fundamental particles in nature, forming bound states such as protons, neutrons, pions and other elementary particles known as hadrons. The study of such particles and their interactions, is called quantum theory (or quantum mechanics). Quarks have been not observed and studied as isolated objects, and according to some theories they cannot be. 

History: 
Quarks have been discovered by a physicist at the California Institute of Technology, Murrary Gell-Mann, who was studying a list of known subatomic particles. As he was studying, he discovered that there is a pattern in each of these units, which he called quarks. He also found three ‘mysterious’ units which he called antiquarks. While Gell-Mann was studying the quarks and antiquarks, he did not think of them as particles and antiparticles. Instead, he thought of them as mathematical rules of existence that lay below the particle world. All the physicists thought that these units were probably some grainy "partons" but in time they’ve discovered that they are not, so they’ve decided to call them Quarks. 
 In time, Gell-Mann divided his three quarks into ‘flavors’. He called them quark flavors ‘up’, ‘down’, and ‘sideways’, abbreviant as u, d, and s.  Furthermore, he said that these three quarks would have the following properties: 

Quark Flavour         Baryon Number        Charge        Strangeness         Spin 
          U                      1/3                         +2/3                   0                   ½ 
          D                      1/3                         -1/3                   0                   ½ 
          S                       1/3                         -1/3                  -1                   ½ 
 Gell-Mann pictured every baryon to be made up of three quarks. For example a proton would consist of quarks u, u, d. The charges of these quarks would add to +1, the baryon number would add to +1 so the resultant strangeness would be 0. Two of the half-spins line up in opposite directions to cancel each other out, leaving the half-spin of the third quark as the spin of the proton. As a result, this arrangement of the particles fits the proton’s properties. 
 On the other hand, neutrons consist of quarks u, d, d. Because of this arrangement, all charges would add up to zero, but all other characteristics would be the same as for the protons. 

Combining Quarks: 
Usually there are three ways to combine quarks. Three quarks all together make a baryon while three antiquarks make an antibaryon. A quark and an antiquark make a meson. Using these different arrangements, hadrons can be constructed. 
 
Hadrons: 
Of the subatomic particles discovered in the period from 1940 to 1975, the vast majority falls into the family of those that interact strongly with one another. These are the hadrons. They include all known particle, except for the photon electron, muon, two neutrinos and their antiparticles. Most of the hadrons are unstable and decay, slowly or rapidly into the few known stable particles. The precise number of known hadrons depends somewhat on the criteria use to distinguish two particles. 

Something about color 
The American physicist Sheldon Glashow suggested that quarks had another quality besides flavor. He called this ‘new’ quality ‘color’. The color of a quark has nothing to do with the usual word ‘color’, this is just another label which scientists gave quarks, in order to identify them easier. Furthermore, Glashow declared that every quark or antiquark was either red or yellow or blue. Because of this mix of three colors results in baryons being ‘colorless’. 

Mesons: 
A meson has only two quarks. For example if one quark is red and one is blue then the meson’s color will be some flavor of ‘nonyellowness’. 

Leptons and photons: 
There is no indication that they have an internal structure. It all seems that if electrons, positrons, muons, neutrinos, and photons are truly basic particles not made of quark or anything else. After tests with X-ray beams on electrons, scientists have concluded that electrons don’t have an internal structure either.  As a result only hadrons fit the quark theory. 

Gluons: are particles which quarks exchange, they are the quarks ‘trade colors’. 

Some of the pion’s and muon’s characteristics are: 
1. The pion is the Yukawa particle. As virtual particles, pions are the agents of the strong force between neighboring nucleons. 
2. Pions are 270 times as massive as normal electrons. There are three kinds of pions: positively, neutral and negatively charged. 
3. A pion has an average lifetime of about one hundred-millionth of a second. When charged pions decay, they usually produce a muon and a neutrino or natineutrino. 
4. The neutrinos that are produced along with muons are not exactly the same as the neutrinos that are produced with electrons during beta decay. The muon neutrino is almost massless, uncharged, it has a little bit of energy and is capable of penetrating 3,500 light years of lead. 
5. There are positively as well as negatively charged muons, but no neutral muons. Muons decay into electrons and neutrinos. Their average lifetime is about a millionth of a second.
